I am a PhD student in Astronomy & Astrophysics at Harvard University.

Using a combination of analytic theory, observations, data visualization, and numerical simulations,
I investigate the structure and dynamics of the local interstellar medium and examine how stars originate from their galactic environment.

Projects


Phase Space Analysis of the Local Interstellar Medium
In collaboration with Alyssa Goodman, Catherine Zucker, Andreas Burkert, João Alves, Michael Foley, Cameren Swiggum

In this work, we study the dynamics of the Radcliffe Wave, a 2.7-kpc-long arrangement of dense gas clouds recently detected via 3D dust mapping. Containing three million solar masses of gas, the Radcliffe Wave comprises the majority of star-forming regions near the Sun including Orion, Cepheus, North America, and Cygnus X. We use 12CO spectral-line observations to obtain the full radial velocity distribution of the cold molecular gas along the Wave’s entire extent. In addition, leveraging the wealth of new data from Gaia DR3, we characterise the 3D space motions of young stellar clusters forming in the Radcliffe Wave, which allows us to trace the motion of its parental gas. By combining the constraints from both spectral-line mapping and the young stellar clusters, we ultimately derive the full 6D phase space signature of the Radcliffe Wave. Given these new dynamical insights, we are able to put constrains on the Wave’s potential origin and the broader implications for the star formation history of the local Milky Way.



A 3D View of Orion: I. Barnard's Loop
In collaboration with Michael Foley, Alyssa Goodman, Catherine Zucker, John Forbes, Cameren Swiggum, João Alves, John Bally, Juan Soler, Josefa Großschedl, Shmuel Bialy, Michael Grudić, Reimar Leike, Torsten Enßlin
Paper

Barnard’s Loop is a famous arc of Hα emission located in the Orion star-forming region. In this work, we investigate possible formation mechanism for Barnard’s Loop. Using 3D dust maps, we characterize the 3D topology of the large-scale Orion region, showing Barnard’s Loop’s correspondence with a large asymmetric dust cavity around the OBP-Near/Briceno-1 (OBP-B1) cluster. The molecular clouds Orion A, Orion B, and Orion λ reside on the shell of this cavity. We find that the large-scale gas in the region appears to be expanding, likely propelled by a combination of feedback from OBP-B1 and the cluster Orion X. Estimates of gravitational effects from both stars and gas indicate that the expansion of this asymmetric cavity likely induced anisotropy in the kinematics of OBP-B1. We conclude that feedback from OBP-B1 has affected the structure of the Orion A, Orion B, and Orion λ molecular clouds and may have played a major role in the formation of Barnard’s Loop.



The Passage of the Solar System through the Local Bubble
In collaboration with Merav Opher, Abraham Loeb, Catherine Zucker, Alyssa Goodman, João Alves, Alexandra Worden, Evan Economo, Jesse Miller, Marc Kornbleuth, Josh Peek

The Sun moves through the interstellar medium (ISM) making the conditions outside the solar system vary with time. Today’s solar system is protected from interstellar particles by the heliosphere, the bubble formed by the solar wind as the Sun moves through the ISM, which engulfs all the planets. However, there is geological evidence from 60Fe that the Earth was in direct contact with the ISM 6-7 million years ago. During the same time, the Sun crossed the edge of the Local Bubble. The expansion of the Local Bubble swept up the ambient ISM into an extended shell that now fragmented and collapsed into the most prominent nearby molecular clouds. Using a state-of-the art magnetohydrodynamic model of the heliosphere, we show that due to the passage of the solar system through the edge of the Local Bubble, the heliosphere is shrunk to a scale smaller than the Earth's orbit (0.7 AU) exposing the Earth to the cold dense ISM, consistent with the evidence of 60Fe. The direct contact of the Earth with the ISM, could have drastic terrestrial effects 6-7 million years ago. In particular, such scenario can be used to explain the cooling seen by Oxygen isotopes measured in deep sea Foraminifera.



The Origin of the Local Interstellar Medium
In collaboration with Catherine Zucker, Sara Starecheski, Seth Redfield, Andreas Burkert

The local interstellar medium (ISM) consists of several warm clouds. In this work, we investigate the effect of the most recent supernova explosion near the Sun – that occurred in the Upper Centaurus Lupus star cluster 1-2 Myr ago – on these local clouds of the ISM. Building on recent analytic frameworks to describe supernova driven bubble expansions, we derive an analytic model for the velocity and position of the local clouds as a function of time, including the cloud deceleration due to the interaction with the ambient medium. We postulate that both the high average speed of the local clouds as well as their direction can be explained by the propagation of this supernova shockwave.
